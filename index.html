How Seller Financing Works

Seller financing is simpler than you might think. Here's a step-by-step guide to how the process works:

1

You set the terms (not the bank)

You and the buyer agree on the purchase price, down payment, interest rate, monthly payment, and how long the loan lasts (often with a 5â€“10 year balloon).

2

You transfer ownership at closing

The buyer gets the deed at closing (just like a normal sale). You are protected by a mortgage or deed of trust recorded against the property.

3

You become the lender

Instead of getting all cash up front, you receive the down payment now and monthly payments from the buyer, including interest.

4

You're secured if something goes wrong

If the buyer stops paying, the agreement includes a deed in lieu of foreclosure clause, so they simply transfer the deed back to you without going through the foreclosure process. The property secures your loan, making it a safe investment.

5

You get paid off later (balloon or refinance)

At the agreed time (often 5â€“7 years), the buyer refinances or sells and pays you the remaining balance in full.

Frequently Asked Questions

Get answers to common questions about seller financing:

Why wouldn't I just wait for a cash buyer?

+

Seller financing often allows you to:

  • Sell at a stronger price
  • Sell faster
  • Receive monthly income
  • Walk away from repairs and management

Many sellers prefer steady income over a lump sum sitting in a low-interest account.

Are there tax benefits?

+

Often, yes. Seller financing may allow you to spread capital gains taxes over time instead of paying them all at once. A CPA can confirm what applies to your situation.

Is this riskier for me than a normal sale?

+

Most of the risk is transferred to the buyer in seller finance.

  • The buyer handles taxes, insurance, repairs, and upkeep
  • You hold a secured claim on the property
  • The monthly payments and all money invested is forfeited in the event of default

You've converted the home into a secured loan, not a liability.

Do I still own the house after closing?

+

No. The buyer becomes the owner at closing, just like a normal sale.

You're protected by a recorded mortgage, giving you the same legal position as a bank until you're paid in full.

What happens if the buyer stops paying?

+

Most seller-financed deals include a deed in lieu of foreclosure.

If payments stop, the property is transferred back to the seller without court involvement or foreclosure.

What would this look like for my specific property?

+

Every seller-financed deal is structured a little differently based on the home, the price, and your goals.
